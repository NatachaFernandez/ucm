# Care Plan as a Living Coordination Construct

## Purpose

This document defines the Care Plan as a foundational concept within the Unified Care Model (UCM).

The Care Plan is not a static document or a single clinical artifact. It is a living coordination construct that supports interdisciplinary collaboration, shared decision making, continuity of care, and achievement of health and wellbeing goals across settings, domains, and time.

This concept applies across healthcare, behavioral health, social care, community services, public health, eligibility and enrollment workflows, and related support ecosystems.

---

# Definition

A Care Plan is a dynamic, evolving representation of an individual’s current state, preferences, goals, priorities, assessments, interventions, services, outcomes, and progress that is created, maintained, exchanged, implemented, monitored, and acted upon by participating actors to coordinate activities and support achievement of desired outcomes.

A Care Plan may exist as a document, data structure, workflow artifact, shared record, or coordinated planning process.

---

# Core Principles

## Living and Evolving

Care Plans continuously evolve as circumstances, evidence, goals, eligibility, risks, interventions, and outcomes change.

Care Plans are expected to support:

* revision
* versioning
* reassessment
* progress tracking
* goal adjustment
* intervention adjustment
* longitudinal updates

---

## Shared Ownership

Care Plans may be owned, contributed to, or governed by multiple actors.

Examples include:

* individuals
* caregivers
* workforce participants
* care teams
* community organizations
* programs
* digital systems

Ownership may vary across contexts.

---

## Interdisciplinary Participation

Care Plans support participation across disciplines and domains.

Examples:

* healthcare
* behavioral health
* social care
* community services
* public health
* eligibility and enrollment
* navigation and coordination

Actors may participate simultaneously or episodically.

---

## Goal-Oriented

Care Plans organize activities around goals.

Goals may include:

* prevention
* stabilization
* recovery
* maintenance
* access
* wellbeing
* participation
* independence
* quality of life

Goals may be short-term, long-term, or continuously evolving.

---

# Care Plan Components

A Care Plan may include:

## Current State

Current understanding of:

* health
* wellbeing
* social circumstances
* strengths
* risks
* barriers
* preferences

---

## Assessments

Structured or unstructured evaluation inputs.

Examples:

* screening
* structured assessment instruments
* observations
* evaluations
* eligibility determination
* risk stratification

Assessment outputs inform planning decisions.

---

## Goals

Desired future outcomes.

Goals may be:

* individual-defined
* caregiver-defined
* team-defined
* jointly negotiated

---

## Interventions

Actions intended to influence outcomes.

Interventions may include:

* evidence-based interventions
* education
* navigation
* referrals
* treatment
* preventive activities
* social interventions
* enrollment activities

---

## Services and Activities

Planned, completed, scheduled, or ongoing activities.

Examples:

* appointments
* referrals
* coordination tasks
* outreach
* monitoring
* follow-up

---

## Outcomes and Progress

Care Plans support measuring:

* completion
* engagement
* effectiveness
* milestones
* outcomes
* changes over time

Progress measurement may trigger updates.

---

# Longitudinal and Episodic Planning

Care Plans may operate across different temporal models.

## Episodic

Focused on a specific event, condition, transition, or period.

Examples:

* discharge planning
* transitional care
* rehabilitation episode

## Longitudinal

Persist across time and evolve continuously.

Examples:

* chronic condition management
* preventive care
* wellness support
* community care coordination

A Care Plan may transition between episodic and longitudinal states.

---

# Preventive Care Planning

Care Plans are not limited to illness.

Healthy individuals may maintain Care Plans that support:

* prevention
* screening
* wellness
* health promotion
* readiness
* life planning
* resilience
* risk reduction

Absence of disease does not imply absence of planning.

---

# Relationship to Coordination

Care Planning and Care Coordination are complementary.

Care Planning establishes:

* goals
* interventions
* expected outcomes

Care Coordination enables:

* execution
* collaboration
* monitoring
* adaptation

Both are iterative and mutually reinforcing.

---

# Implementation Considerations

Implementation may include:

* structured data
* interoperable exchange
* version control
* auditability
* permissions
* collaboration workflows
* measurement frameworks

Representation is implementation-specific.

The Care Plan concept remains implementation independent.
